NEW ANCIENT (2020) by Avi Goldman
NEW ANCIENT (2020) by Avi Goldman
The objects created in this sculpture above are made out of fungal mycelium.




“…when they excavate the modern day
they’ll find us: The Brand New Ancients.
See — all that we have here is all that we’ve always had.”

-K Tempest, The Brand New Ancients




NEW ANCIENT is an electronic sculptural installation grown and assembled out of fungus. The project grew out of my inspiration from the branching, networking, fibers of fungal mycelium. Hidden long underground, mycelium has thrived since prehistoric times, as a very vital life force of the terrestrial ecology.

Despite being so archaic and integral, mycelia have only been recently uncovered by humans. This discovery is quite significant, and peculiar. Mycelia baffle humans with their almost bodiless expression of interconnectedness, antithetical to our highly accepted conceptions of individual separateness. Without an inside or outside, or a definitive shape, mycelia are weave together chaotic entanglement of life, and inspire a sense of interconnectedness. They invoke a sense of ecological boundary-lessness. Scientists have discovered mycelia have a key role in conducting the cycles of the forests; decaying organic material and breaking down rock, funneling nutrients, and forming complex relationships with minerals, plants and animals. The discovery of such an orchestration also perplexingly clashes with our very human conceptions about ‘intelligence.’ We now understand that the lives of mycelium ultimately provide the very medium of soil that life on land lives in, through, and on.

Having been so inspired by mycelium, I set out to build something that would allow me to interact with it in practice, and also expose myself to new ways of thinking. How does this movement of earthen life through eons result in an anthropocene, with human beings manipulating nature, as if within some grand delusion of separation? The project strives to reconnect with this primordial form in new and unexpected ways by combining organic and digital media, unified in one expression.



The sculpture came about from my practice of experimentation with a new material called ‘myco-composite.’ This material is an ecological plastic alternative, a composite made from recycled debris held together by mycelium. The mycelium is given food and water and entangles itself to fill the space within a mold, ultimately forming a foam-like material. I grew this living material to envelope electronic elements which perform a dance of live circuitry. While the practices of this work has an inherit ecological spirit, the exploration embraces artificial materials, and integrates them into the sculpture as whole, in a catalyzing expression. The sculpture plays within the themes of cyber punk, and further, riffs eco punk, in a post industrial unification of nature and technology.

Essentially, the sculpture I crafted is a cyborg, but not in the traditional sense. Its inanimate structural medium is living fungus, and its active electric expression comes from inert plastic metal, and semiconductor. The sculpture is not a robot, or a system with a task, rather a living work of art with metaphorical conveyance. My goal was to invite the viewer to frolic in paradox.

I grew fungal material into the form of several skulls, a living amorphous material formed into an iconic significance of death. Circuitry pop out of the eye sockets of the skulls, which bring these passive objects into a spark to life! Each positioned side by side, diode eyes lights up, one by one, in a rapid unified sweep. These discrete skulls are acting though the same circuit in unison, blurring the perception of individuality. Together in close communion, they watch an LCD monitor in front of them. On the screen is a visualization of a radial mycelium network, bifurcating outwards from a central point. I developed this code based on a mathematical principle of how chaos organizes itself in a natural branching pattern. Every iteration of the code grows a completely unique digital mycelium in real time. The screen is housed in a monolithic rectangular block of mycocomposite. Wires protruding from the back of skulls tangle up in a mess and plug into this main module of microprocessors. Stepping back, the overall performance represents a recursive dance which eludes to some sort of self reflectance or ‘consciousness.’ The skulls representatively ‘watch’ the computer process new possible networks. Such an anthropomorphic ambiguity evokes that these active skulls could even be behind the activity of processing in an electrical brainstorm. I would hope one would allow themselves to imaginatively question where such a process begins, or whether there is an entity behind the play, finally reflecting this within ones own self consciousness.



For me, the piece became a practice and an exploration of a recursive dialectic, which explodes dualistic assumptions. Interrelated frames consume their own otherness into the same mystery: Distinctions such as, alive versus intent, natural versus synthetic, emergent innocence versus intended appliance, chaos and order…, dance around each, and inclusively embrace each other in inconclusive annihilation.

By experimenting with the use of this very archaic form of life, mycelium, as a technological application that does not exclude itself from the greater ecology, we are performing this new embrace.

On the crest of the post industrial era, we must reflect on the sense of separateness that humanity has come to believe that it is : Who, or what, is truly behind this human drive that has unequivocally changed ‘Nature’? And does this labeled macro-activity really stand apart from ‘Nature’?



This project doesn’t offer questions and not answers. The sculpture, and the subsequent thinking and writing, serves as a substrate to play in, beyond the bounds of the limitation of just completely knowing. Within this illumination, a releases can be made into new possibilities for appreciating what we call organic or technological, natural or altered, and ultimately recall that we have reasoned distinctions —yet all is not necessarily as it seems. This is a reflection which is imperative in an age in which humanity must recognize its inseparability from its environment and adapt or face grave consequences.

Yet, while the bravado of the technological age is the discovery of new immediate solutions, there is importance within the context of making a new shift, of truly finding humility. We believe, so cleverly, in accepted definitions that lose sense upon investigation. We are deluded enough to truly think we know exactly what nature is or isn’t, or what technology definitively includes, and that limits us from the humility of looking beyond ourselves for answers. We must be willing to do things like search for new answers and new questions from our ancient co-inhabitants of Earth and not just within our own models language and of life. We must be able to form new connections that break down the axiomatic walls of our own networks of understanding and defining. And while unknowing is scary, this is how we can form relationships and systems anew, and build new life out of the destruction of forms once assumed.
